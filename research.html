China's Technological Spillover Effect on the Energy Efficiency of the BRI Countries

with Oluwasegun B. Adekoya, Johnson A. Oliyide, and Oluwademilade T. Kenku.

Energy Policy, 182, 113740, 2023.

Abstract

Given the rising technological progress and infrastructural developments among the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) countries and the global move towards a reduction in the use of fossil fuels to mitigate climate change, this study aims to examine the role of technological innovation in the countries' energy efficiency. We discover that energy intensity is increased by endogenous technological innovation in the BRI countries, especially at the middle and higher quantiles. We then remove China from the panel in order to check if the country's high technological advancement and energy efficiency might have an outlying influence on the relationship. Except for a relative decline in the significant estimates across the quantiles, the direction of effect largely remains. Finally, China's technological innovation has a reducing spillover effect on the energy intensity of the BRI countries. These findings indicate that, while endogenous technological innovations are detrimental to the energy efficiency of the BRI countries, China's technologies are energy efficient for the other BRI countries, and should be adopted or absorbed to reduce dependence on fossil fuels.

Landlord Responsiveness to Eviction Filing Fees: Evidence from Northern New England

with Kelsi G. Hobbs and E. Wibabara.

Regional Studies, Regional Science, 13(1), 2620937, 2026.

Abstract

US landlords file millions of evictions per year. Eviction is associated with numerous negative outcomes including economic hardship, residential instability, disrupted educational attainment among children, and physical and mental health issues. Despite the number of evictions and eviction’s negative consequences, there is little research suggesting effective eviction prevention policies. One potential way to reduce evictions is to increase the costs of eviction to landlords. This paper explores landlord responsiveness to eviction filing fees. Using data from northern New England, we find that landlords' responsiveness to changes in eviction filing fees is relatively inelastic. As a result, increasing costs to landlords through higher filing fees may be an inefficient method of preventing evictions.

Forecasting Average Retail Electricity Prices Delivered to the Residential Sector in Maine

Status: Working paper / Available at SSRN

Abstract

This study employs the Box-Jenkins methodology to forecast average retail electricity prices delivered to the residential sector in Maine from 2024 to 2025. The methodology involves data transformation, model identification, estimation, diagnostic checking, and forecasting. Initially, the data are inspected for stationarity and transformed as necessary, with the Box-Cox transformation applied to stabilize variance. Unit root tests are then conducted to assess stationarity, followed by selecting the most appropriate ARIMA model based on information criteria. The chosen model is used to generate forecasts, taking into account both nominal and real prices adjusted for inflation. Results indicate an increasing trend in electricity prices, highlighting the importance of energy efficiency measures to mitigate the financial burden on residents. While the study provides valuable insights, it acknowledges that a univariate analysis is not very adequate to make an accurate forecast of the average electricity prices delivered in Maine; hence, multivariate analysis such as ARIMA-X, ARDL, and others could be used to forecast residential electricity prices in Maine in future research.

Dynamic Spillovers Among Dirty Energy Market and Stocks of Leading Emitters of Carbon: Evidence from the Novel Quantile Time-Frequency Approach

with Johnson A. Oliyide, Sodiq A. Oladipupo, Oluwafemi David Ogunjemilua, and Abdulmuttolib Salako.

Status: Working paper / Available at SSRN

Abstract

This study utilizes the quantile time-frequency connectedness approach developed by Chatziantoniou et al. (2022) to analyze the interconnectedness and spillover of shocks between fossil fuels and the stocks of major carbon emitters worldwide. By combining the quantile VAR connectedness method of Chatziantoniou et al. (2022), the time connectedness approach of Diebold and Yilmaz (2012, 2014), and the frequency connectedness analysis of Baruník and Křehlík (2018), we examine the shock transmission among crude oil, natural gas, and the stocks of the United States, China, Japan, and India. Daily data from January 4, 2010, to March 23, 2023, is employed for this purpose.

The empirical findings emphasize the importance of understanding connectedness beyond the median quantile and time domain for investment strategies, as market risks tend to be more pronounced during extreme market states, and short-term shocks drive the spillover of shocks. In terms of median connectedness, the United States emerges as the dominant spillover transmitter, exerting the largest influence on overall short-term and long-term risk. Japan, on the other hand, stands out as the primary net receiver of shocks in both the short and long term, while crude oil and natural gas exhibit a net receiving role. However, during extreme market states and across different frequencies, the receptiveness and transmission nature of assets change.

Besides short-term shocks, significant risk flows occur during various crisis periods, including the COVID-19 pandemic. Furthermore, a heatmap analysis of total connectedness indicates that the spillover of shocks is higher during positive returns in the short term, whereas the long-term analysis contradicts this finding. Finally, the study offers insightful implications for investment strategies and policy recommendations based on the results obtained.
